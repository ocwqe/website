Computers in the workforce

Computers are essential in the workplace, as they increase the efficiency and effectiveness of work tenfold. They are most often used for data storage, access to certain software such as Microsoft excel and communication software such as Microsoft Teams. Typically, computers are traditionally seen as just a laptop or pc, however, people forget that simple things such as cash registers and fingerprint scanners are also computers!

Some prominent examples of computers being used in the workforce are:

  • Hospitals; They utilise computers to store information about patients such as their age, proportions, medical history, and what medicine they are taking.
  • Libraries; Libraries use computers to store where all the books in the library are, and a computer scans books and your library card if you are taking a book or giving one back.
  • Stores; Stores use computers in the form of (self)chechouts, which scan items and calculate the amount needed, and the change.

Advantages and disadvantages

There are many more advantages to having computers and technology as a whole in the workplace than there are disadvantages, some of which include;

  • Automated customer support tools.
  • Allows for greater mobility and flexibility, as some tasks may now be completed solely online.
  • Encourages creativity and efficiency, as companies can compete to make the best and most innovative designs/systems.

However, there are still flaws to having more technology and computers in the workplace, some of which include;

  • May encourage laziness.
  • It may cause distractions at work.
  • May result in breaches of personal privacy.

Artificial intelligence

AI is rapidly growing in influence and popularity, with AI such as ChatGPT showing the public at large just what current Artificial Intelligence is capable of. However, AI has been essential in business and technology for years now, with businesses utilising it to automate processes, surveil security and to forecast sales. Additionally, AI can even be found on your phone in the form of predicitive text, phone AI's and even face ID!

With how popular AI has grown over the past few years, we must ask ourselves; is it really beneficial, or are we just setting ourselves up to be easy prey for our future AI overlords.. Luckily, AI holds no real power over us just yet..

Advantages and disadvantages

Here is a quick breakdown of what AI can do, and how it can be both a benefit and a detriment to us.

  • AI is used in search engines to give relevant searches to its user, and it accomplishes this by analysing the users previous searches.
  • Language translation software uses AI to translate from one language to another.
  • AI can be used in manufacturing via robots, which greatly increases efficiency.
  • You can have AI in games to compete against players.

AI can be both extraordinarily beneficial and detrimental, making it a very sharp double edged sword. For example, even though AI greatly increases efficiency, it may also result in the loss of jobs for millions, as they get replaced by faster working and less needy machines. Yet another example can be found within gaming, where AI can be used to make bots which can make games more engaging and interesting, however, AI technology can also be used maliciously by players of said game, with them making bots which possess superhuman levels of skill, and making them look like a normal player in game so they can be used in online matches, making it unfair for other players.

Automated Decision Making (ADM)

ADM is being used more and more with the advent of more advanced technology, with it now being used in debates to assess arguments and view the quality of debates and being used to supplement or replace the human judgment of judges, civil servants and police officers in many contexts. ADM is closely linked to the field of Artificial Intelligence, with AI commonly utilising ADM in contexts such as predicitve analytics and user profiling. Most commonly, ADM is used in smaller decisions, however, bigger issues may arise if it is used in the decision making of bigger more impactful choices.

Concerns with ADM

The most common concerns with ADM are;

  1. A lack of transparency and contestability of decisions, incursions on privacy and surveillance.
  2. Incursions on privacy and surveillance.
  3. Exacerbating systemic bias and inequality due to data and algorithmic bias.
  4. The spread of misinformation via media platforms and administrative discrimination

Despite these drawbacks, ADM is still very widely used, as it increases efficiency exponentially, and can be used in tandem with AI for even better results. ADM will most likely be used more and more as technology (especially AI) progresses, but we will still try to minimise the risks as much as possible.

Environmental effects

Our growing need for technology has resulted in many negative effects on the environments, as to supply our demand of technology we have had to build 100's of new factories to construct our hardware, resulting in increased greenhouse gas emissions, which contributes to climate change. Additionally, it has seen us deplete the natural resources of our planet even faster than ever before, and has resulted in the destruction of many natural habitats as we attempt to free up space for our roads, homes and railways.

While technology has resulted in many negative effects for the natural environment, it has also increased human quality of life exponentially, with better transportation being available, better housing, more enjoyable recreation and bulk production of many essential and important products such as food and mobile devices.

Censorship and the internet.

Censorship on the internet has the potential of being used for good, such as when websites don't allow youths to access content they shouldnt be able to, and stopping people from seeing messages which may incite violence. It also has the potential of being used maliciously, as it may limit peoples access to important info and skills, which may lead to less opportunities for said person. In addition to this, it may stifle creativity and critical thinking, as it may stop people from expressing their opinions , leading to only one side of an argument being represented fairly online, leading to misinformation and false beliefs spreading.

Monitoring behaviour.

Monitoring behaviour is also a double edged sword, with it potentially being used both for good and bad. It is traditionally used within schools to keep tabs on a students progress and behaviour during their academic journey, which is useful as it makes all of that information ready to be seen on demand, and is a convenient way of storing said info. However, monitoring behaviour may extend to the monitoring of an individuals behaviour online, and problems may arise in that scenario, as sites may store sensitive info about peoples behaviour that they would rather not have stored. This may be seen as a breach of privacy by many. Typically, sites do this to provide predicitive links and results to an individual based on their previous behaviour, such as relevant websites and searches.

Piracy and offensive communications.

Piracy is the unauthorised copying of software, with the most common form of piracy being gaining access to entertainment media without paying. Rates of piracy are growing by the minute, and this is due to how:

  1. Easy it is.
  2. Only mild consequences follow if you are caught.
  3. It is more convenient.
Some groups of people have even formed communities solely dedicated to piracy, such as the infamous Pirate Bay.

Offensive communications are the act of harassing another person. The internet has escalated this issue greatly, as users may harass people anonymously, and across the world. Luckily, There are laws in place to help prevent offensive communications. Some of these include:

  • Section 1 of the Malicious Communications Act 1998, which says it is an offence to send an indecent, offensive or threatening letter, electronic communication or other article to another person.
  • Section 85 of the Communications Act 2003 and Section 85 of the Postal Services Act 2000 which detail similar specific offences related to sending postal or telephone messages which are indecent, offensive or threatening
Both offences are punishable with up to six months imprisonment and/or a fine. Because the Malicious Communications Offence covers more areas than the Telecommunications offence it is more likely to be used by the Police than the Postal Services or Communications Act offences.

User Interface Design Considerations.

There are many factors to consider then designing a UI, such as the layout, colours, and character sets. Ideally, you want an intuitive layout with colours which work together perfectly and a universal design (one which is able to be used and understood by all). A universal design may be achieved by, for example, having large font sizes, which helps the visually impaired read through your site, or avoiding voice interactable UI's, as they may not recognise certain accents and/or voices
